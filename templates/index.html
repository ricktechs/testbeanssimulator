Case Studies

Threshers that use fossils fuels such as grain combine harvesters have become popular because they overcome difficulties associated with traditional method of threshing harvested beans. The conventional methods basically involve beating them with a pestle or stick and bullock treading which are inefficient as they requires a lot of energy and results in a lot of breakages and losses. However, the existing economic structure of agricultural production together with small size of farms in developing countries like Kenya makes their use uneconomical.

The gap can be filled by stationary threshers that use clean energy such as solar which is renewable although with challenges that requires improvement. Grain damage caused by impacts of the moving parts of crop threshing units and incorrect clearances between the stationary and moving parts of the units are some of the concerns to bean farmers and consumers. Higher thresher unit rotor speeds favour higher threshing efficiencies, throughput and output capacities; but results in an increased seed damage as high as 40-50%. There is also a problem of separation of the seed from the straws which is related to concave sieve sizes.

Finally, very little information is available about the common bean characteristic, machine and operational parameters in relation to the performance of the threshing unit. Therefore, bean variety, moisture content, feed rate, cylinder peripheral speeds, concave configurations and clearance have to be studied. Modelling of threshing and separation process, threshing loss and grain damage will provide a better understanding of the fundamental relationship of different machine and crop characteristics in order to identify the contribution of each variable.
